Interpretation

Note: A Gini coefficient of 0 represents perfect equality, while 1 represents maximum inequality.

How the Gini Coefficient Works

The Gini coefficient is a measure of statistical dispersion used to represent income inequality within a nation or group. It was developed by Italian statistician Corrado Gini in 1912.

Calculation Method

The Gini coefficient is calculated as the ratio of the area between the line of equality and the Lorenz curve to the total area under the line of equality:

G = (A) / (A + B)

Where A is the area between the line of equality and the Lorenz curve, and B is the area under the Lorenz curve.

Interpretation Guide

Inequality Levels

  • 0.00 - 0.25: Very Low Inequality
  • 0.25 - 0.35: Low Inequality
  • 0.35 - 0.45: Moderate Inequality
  • 0.45 - 0.60: High Inequality
  • 0.60 - 1.00: Very High Inequality

Country Examples

  • Denmark (0.25): Very equal distribution
  • Germany (0.32): Low inequality
  • United States (0.41): Moderate inequality
  • Brazil (0.53): High inequality
  • South Africa (0.63): Very high inequality

Tips for Accurate Analysis

  • • Ensure income data is comprehensive and representative
  • • Consider using household income rather than individual income
  • • Account for regional cost of living differences
  • • Include all income sources (wages, investments, transfers)
  • • Use consistent time periods for comparisons
  • • Consider complementary measures like the Palma ratio
